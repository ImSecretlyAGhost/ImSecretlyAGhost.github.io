Chicago Housing Conditions, VIII: Slovaks in the Twentieth Ward

Authors: Helen L. Wilson and Eunice W. Smith

Source: American Journal of Sociology, Vol. 20, No. 2, September 1914, pp. 145-169

Overview

The Twentieth Ward of Chicago, particularly the blocks between Sixteenth and Eighteenth streets and Union to Burlington, was predominantly inhabited by Slovaks. This district represented a newer wave of Slavic immigration, with Slovaks occupying one-third of households, along with Lithuanians, Bohemians, and Poles. The area had a long history of poor infrastructure and overcrowding.

Housing and Population

  • Total households surveyed: 854
  • Children under 12: ~32% of the population
  • Adult lodgers: 13%
  • Population density: Average 287 persons per acre
  • Most houses: 1–2 stories, under 5 apartments each
  • Majority of houses (73%) were frame structures, old, and in disrepair

Sanitation and Toilets

Privacy and convenience were major issues, especially for the sick, elderly, or families with children.

Overcrowding and Apartment Conditions

  • 53% of sleeping rooms were overcrowded according to legal standards
  • Ceilings often below legal height; cellars and basements were damp and unhealthy
  • Windows inadequate for light and ventilation; many opened onto lot-lines or passages
  • Boarders often worsened overcrowding and lack of privacy

Rents and Ownership

  • Median rent for a four-room apartment: $9–$9.50, comparable to South Chicago workers
  • 17% of apartments owned by occupants; the remainder rented
  • Tenancy duration linked to ownership: 37% of owners lived in their homes for over 15 years

Conclusion

Residents of the Slovak district faced long-term challenges including overcrowding, poor sanitation, inadequate ventilation, and old, deteriorating housing. These conditions highlight systemic issues in urban housing for immigrant populations in early 20th century Chicago.
